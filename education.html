Courses

  • SI106: Introduction to Python:

    • An introduction to programming with a focus on applications in informatics using Python.
    • Focused on fundamental elements of a modern programming language and how to access data on the Internet (API, JSON, etc.).
    • Explored how humans and technology complement one another, including techniques used to coordinate groups of people working together on software development.
    • Test and debug the program using rigorous techniques.
    • Introduction to programming and the culture of programming, with a focus on applications for people, by people, and about people.
    • Focused on applications for end-users or analysts, as opposed to back-end or infrastructure software and processing data traces of people’s actions and interactions.

      • Introduction to key concepts in the field and focus on five major health informatics domains:
      1. Health Information Infrastructure
      2. Biomedical Research Informatics
      3. Clinical Health Informatics
      4. Consumer Health Informatics
      5. Public Health Informatics
      • Focused on a variety of topics and methods that pertain to the field of health informatics:
      1. Healthcare Data (EHR), Standards, and Design
      2. Health Equity and Health Policy
      3. Health IT Management
      4. Health Informatics Evaluation
      5. Computable Knowledge Management

  • EECS280: Programming and Introductory Data Structures:

    • Take a problem and consider various possible approaches for solving it.
    • Select an approach or algorithm that provides for a simple, clean, well-structured solution.
    • Convert the algorithm into C++ code, using good design and style.
    • Test and debug the program using rigorous techniques.
    • Understand the concepts of top-down design, data encapsulation, information hiding, and procedural and data abstraction.
    • Design, implement and use complete classes, including constructors, destructors, and operator overloading.
    • Implement dynamic data structures for stacks, queues and lists.
    • Be able to quickly design, implement, test and debug a large scale project independently.

      1. Data Cleaning
      2. Data Manipulation
      3. Data Visualization
      4. Statistical Modeling

  • STATS401: Applied Statistical Methods II:

    • An intermediate course in Applied Statistics focusing on the linear model and its applications.
    • Data analyses in applications drawn across the social sciences, the natural sciences, and public data.
      • Focused on a variety of topics and methods that pertain to the field of data science and statistics:
      1. Simple Linear Regression, Two-sample Problems, One-way Analysis of variance (ANOVA)
      2. Multiple Linear Regression, Diagnostics and Model Selection
      3. Two-way Analysis of Variance, Multiple Comparisons using R

Academic Projects

Engineers Make a Difference (Growing Hope):

Worked with a team of four to plan, design, build, and test a raised bed and vertical garden for plantation and a water catchment system to recycle the rain to increase self-sustainability Gained manufacturing and system engineering skills such as machining and project management Presented the design and project to Growing Hope to promote healthy eating and self-sustainable gardens to inner city households with low income

Accent Hallucination:

Implicit bias is defined as referring to the attitudes or stereotypes that affect our understanding, actions, and decisions in an unconscious manner. The focus of my project was on the implicit bias of race and how it affects people audio and interpretation. To be quite precise, I basically replicated Donald Rubin’s studies of NONLANGUAGE FACTORS AFFECTING UNDERGRADUATES' JUDGMENTS OF NONNATIVE ENGLISH-SPEAKING TEACHING ASSISTANTS. Thus, the basis of my project was the Rubin’s experiment, and I have also taken a little bit of basic concept and idea from McGowan’s Social Expectation Improves Speech Perception in Noise.
